A Picture is Worth A Thousand Words

By Kendall Leonard, Tatum Good, and Dr. Heather Kitada Smalley

Exploring the Relationship Between COVID-19 & Data Visualizations: A Graduate Research Project

Overview

During the COVID-19 pandemic, visualizations became one of the most important tools for understanding the world. From dashboards tracking cases to animated charts in the New York Times, these graphics became part of our daily lives‚Äîsometimes shaping behavior as much as the underlying data itself. This project explores how visual storytelling evolved under crisis conditions, and how that evolution impacted how we viewed the pandemic.

We analyze 173 data-driven articles from the ‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs Going On in This Graph?‚Äù series published between 2015 and 2024. Our goal was to explore how the urgency, complexity, and volume of pandemic data reshaped visual design practices‚Äîboth in terms of graphic form (e.g., interactivity, color use, complexity) and thematic focus (e.g., health and risk communication). We also wished to explore how these changes under pressure may have affected how we viewed the pandemic. By combining historical analysis, literature review, and our own empirical study, we provide a timeline of this transformation and reflect on its broader implications.

Data Process

We began by collecting all available ‚ÄúWhat‚Äôs Going On in This Graph?‚Äù articles published by the New York Times Learning Network in collaboration with the American Statistical Association. These weekly publications were designed to promote visual literacy in classrooms, but they also provide a consistent, publicly accessible record of data visualizations in media from 2015‚Äì2024.

After compiling 173 articles, we manually coded metadata for each graphic: type (bar, line, scatter, etc.), topic (e.g., health, economy, sports), presence of interactivity, design complexity, and use of Gestalt principles. Each graphic was also analyzed for its interpretive demands‚Äîsuch as number of perception tasks required and whether uncertainty (e.g., confidence intervals) was visually communicated.

To assess changes over time, we split the dataset into pre-COVID (before March 2020) and post-COVID subsets. We are using R to compute trends and cross-tabulations‚Äîlooking not only at which graphic forms became more common, but also how their design adapted in response to pandemic-related content.
